# Subject Line: 2-3 Words

Keep subject lines **2-3 words maximum** that could reasonably be sent by a customer or colleague. Simple, conversational, thought-provoking. Avoid personalization tokens ({{first_name}}, {{company_name}}) - data shows they decrease responses.

## Core Principles

- 2-3 words maximum
- Should look like colleague or customer email
- Thought-provoking or relevant to business
- Test classics against clever ideas (classics often win)
- Avoid spam keywords (free, guarantee, limited time)
- Lavender data shows personalization tokens hurt performance

## When to Use

Use 2-3 word subjects when:
- Launching any cold email campaign
- Want to maximize open rates
- Testing new messaging
- Blending into prospect's inbox
- Following proven best practices

## How to Apply

**The Classics (Always Test These):**

- "quick question"
- "question"
- "question for {{first_name}}" (despite personalization data, still test)
- "intro [company] <> [your company]"
- "intro [first_name] <> [your name]"

**Pro tip:** "People love to hate on 'quick question' subject line but very often, these subject lines perform better than others. There's a reason they are so popular."

**Pattern-Based:**

**Single Word:**
- "question"
- "growth"
- "list?"
- "partnership"

**Two Words:**
- "cheers [to the role]"
- "your customers"
- "more customers"
- "sales deliverability"
- "[problem keyword]"

**Three Words:**
- "question for [first_name]"
- "you and [leader]'s goals"
- "emails in spam?"
- "more customers like [case study]?"

**Context-Specific:**
If campaign references specific topic, use that:
- "data silos?" (for data integration product)
- "domain age" (for email deliverability)
- "review cleanup" (for reputation management)

**Testing Strategy:**

**Test 1:** Classic ("quick question")
**Test 2:** Relevant keyword (problem you solve)
**Test 3:** Clever/unique angle

Often Test 1 wins. Clever doesn't always beat proven.

**Personalization Token Warning:**

**Lavender data suggests:** Personalizing with {{company_name}} or {{first_name}} in subject **decreases** responses.

**GEX position:** "We haven't seen a huge issue with it but I'd want to make that known."

**Safe approach:** Test with and without. Don't assume personalization = better.

**What Makes a Good Subject:**

✓ **Could be from colleague:**
"Question about [topic]" ← Yes
"EXCLUSIVE OFFER FOR YOU" ← No

✓ **Could be from customer:**
"List?" ← Yes
"Re: Your Request" (when there was no request) ← No

✓ **Relevant to business context:**
"Sales deliverability" ← Yes (if they do sales)
"Pizza orders" ← No (unless actually about pizza)

✓ **Curiosity without clickbait:**
"Cheers to the role" ← Yes (if they're new in role)
"You won't believe this!" ← No

**Examples in Context:**

**GEX Template 1:**
```
Subject: cheers to the role

Hey {{first_name}} - LinkedIn told me you took over the VP of Sales role...
```

**GEX Template 2:**
```
Subject: more customers like {{case study}}?

Saw that work you did with {{case study}}...
```

**GEX Template 3:**
```
Subject: list?

Hey {{first_name}} – saw you had SDRs on the team...
```

**GEX Template 4:**
```
Subject: emails in spam?

Hey {{first_name}} – saw you had SDRs and had to figure you were doing cold emailing...
```

**Common Mistake - Too Clever:**

Bad:
- "🎯 Increase your pipeline by 300%!"
- "RE: Our conversation" (when there wasn't one)
- "URGENT: Check this out"
- "Limited time offer for {{company}}"

These scream "sales email" and trigger spam filters.
