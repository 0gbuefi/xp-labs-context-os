# Pain Point Reframing (Josh Braun Method)

Dramatically increase response rates by **changing the assumption** about prospect's situation. Instead of "you need our service," assume they already have a solution and question whether it's optimized.

## Core Principles

- Most prospects already have a solution to their problem
- Asking "do you need X?" assumes they don't have X (usually wrong)
- Asking "how do you know your current X is working?" surfaces doubt
- Reframe positions you as second opinion, not replacement
- Small copy change (under 15 words) can transform results

## When to Use

Use this reframing when:
- Prospects likely already have incumbent solution
- Initial "do you need X?" messaging fails
- Want to bypass "we're happy with current solution" objection
- Selling into established category with competitors
- Positioning as audit/review rather than replacement

## How to Apply

**The Pattern:**

**BEFORE (Assumes they need you):**
"Do you need help with [problem]?"

**AFTER (Assumes they have solution, questions quality):**
"How do you know your current [solution] is [achieving optimal outcome]?"

**Example 1: Tax Accountant**

**Before:**
"Hey, if you need to get your taxes done, we can help you. We have a lot of experience doing people's taxes. Let me know if you need help."

Result: OK performance

**After (Josh Braun tweak):**
"How do you know your current tax accountant is using every legal loophole to get you as much back from the government as possible?"

**Fundamental shift:**
1. Changed assumption: they DON'T have accountant → they DO have one
2. Pressed pain point: no one really knows if they're getting every deduction
3. Positioning: second opinion, not replacement

Result: Dramatic response rate increase

**Example 2: Sales CRM**

**Before:**
"Do you need better sales commission tracking?"

**After:**
"How do you keep track of varying sales commission rates for each role and their accelerators?"

Makes them think about complexity they're currently managing manually.

**The Underlying Pattern:**

When running outbound, you're making assumptions about the market. In tax example:
- **Implicit assumption:** They don't have an accountant
- **Better assumption:** They probably already work with someone

Question your underlying assumptions when things aren't working.

**Process:**
1. Identify your current messaging assumption
2. Write down: "We assume prospects [assumption]"
3. Flip the assumption: "What if prospects already [have solution]?"
4. Craft question that creates doubt about current solution
5. Test reframed version against original

**Formula:**
```
"How do you [verify/ensure/know] your current [solution] is [optimal outcome] without [common gap]?"

Examples:
- "How do you know your wash mitt won't scratch your car?" (car care)
- "How do you know you're not being overcharged for shipping?" (logistics)
- "How do you know violations aren't occurring between MVR pulls?" (fleet safety)
```
