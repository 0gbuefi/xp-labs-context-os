# Staying Close to Their Words

The most powerful copy technique to come out of online market research is deceptively simple: **use their language, not yours**. Specifically, write copy that stays very close to the actual phrases the audience uses to describe their own experience — without copying anyone verbatim.

When done correctly, readers react with "how do you know me so well?" — a visceral recognition that the copy is describing *their* experience, not a generic version of it. This reaction is only possible when the copy is grounded in real audience language, not invented by a copywriter.

This is also called the "kind of thing I wouldn't get away with in high school English" approach — colloquial, specific, sometimes grammatically loose, but emotionally precise.

## Core Principles

- Use their vocabulary, not industry vocabulary or expert vocabulary
- Combine language from multiple sources to create something original but true to the market
- Specific sensory and physical language is more powerful than conceptual language
- The goal is recognition, not originality — readers should think "that's exactly it" not "wow, clever"
- Don't copy one person verbatim; blend multiple voices into a composite that's authentically theirs

## When to Use

Apply this technique when:
- Writing opening lines for cold emails
- Crafting pain-agitation sections
- Writing subject lines about a specific problem
- Creating any copy that needs to trigger recognition from the audience
- Translating research findings into actual email or sales page language

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Collect Multiple Versions

Before writing, gather 3-5 different ways the audience has described the same thing:
- "slaps me straight in my eyeball"
- "a wave washing over me"
- "I get this cold rush from head to toe"
- "electric feeling in my hands"
- "like someone shot cold fluid into my veins"

These are all describing the same thing (physical anxiety sensation) in different ways.

### Step 2: Synthesize, Don't Copy

Pick the most vivid elements from multiple sources and combine:
- Take "cold rush from head to toe" (accurate, physical)
- Add "like someone shot cold fluid into my veins" (visceral, specific)
- Blend: "I get this cold rush from head to toe, like someone shot cold fluid into my veins"

You've created something new that no one said, but that sounds exactly like something they would say.

### Step 3: Stay Colloquial

Research language is rarely polished. Keep that rawness:
- "it hits me right in the face" > "I experience anxiety immediately upon waking"
- "I don't want to talk to anyone" > "social withdrawal is common"
- "does anyone else feel this" > "many people with anxiety report"

The colloquial version converts. The clinical version doesn't.

### Step 4: Test for the Recognition Reaction

Read your copy out loud from the target audience's perspective. Does it feel like:
- "this person has been in my head" → yes, use it
- "this is a reasonable description of my situation" → no, go back to the research
