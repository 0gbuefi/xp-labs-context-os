# Preventative Product Risk Reframe

## The Problem This Solves

**"People don't buy preventatives — they buy cucumbers."**

Preventative products (alarm systems, insurance, guardrails, health screenings) don't cure anything. They stop something bad from happening before it happens. By definition, the prospect hasn't felt the pain the product prevents — so there's no felt problem to sell against.

The standard copywriting approach (lead with the problem) seems to break down.

It doesn't. The problem just needs to be reframed.

---

## The Core Reframe

**The problem isn't the event the product prevents. The problem is living with the unprotected risk of that event.**

The risk is happening *right now*. Every moment the prospect doesn't have the product, they are carrying an active, current problem: exposure to a bad outcome they haven't protected against.

This reframe is not manipulation — it's accurate. A homeowner without an alarm system genuinely has the problem of living with the unprotected risk of break-in. A parent without bed guardrails genuinely has the problem of their child sleeping at fall risk. Making them aware of that is doing them a favor, not frightening them dishonestly.

---

## The Two-Step Technique

### Step 1: Identify the Real Problem (The Risk They're Carrying)

Work backwards from what the product prevents:
- Alarm system → prevents violent home break-in → current problem = unprotected risk of violent home break-in
- Bed guardrail → prevents child falling from bed → current problem = child sleeping at fall risk every night
- Insurance → prevents financial ruin from incident → current problem = unprotected financial exposure

**The problem is not hypothetical.** It exists right now, tonight, every time they sleep. The prospect just hasn't thought about it.

### Step 2: Show Them the Conclusion (The Dark Future Scenario)

Bring the prospect into a specific, vivid, situational future that shows what happens if the risk materializes.

This is an extension of [[specific-situation-beats-generic-pain]]: you're not describing "a break-in could happen." You're placing the reader *inside the moment* with full sensory and emotional detail.

**What makes a dark future scenario effective:**
- **Specific scene-setting** (time of night, who's home, where everyone is)
- **Sensory detail** (the thud, the scream, the sound of breaking glass)
- **The identity wound** (he's lying there feeling like a failure as a father; she's holding her baby wondering if she's the worst mother)
- **The consequence** (they steal everything / the child is unconscious / the ambulance arrives)

The identity wound is key — it connects the risk event to one of the deep buying motivations from [[deep-buying-motivations-master-list]] (#1 identity, #3 guilt, #14 self-defense). This is what makes the scenario emotionally real, not just intellectually frightening.

## Worked Examples

### Home Alarm System

**Wrong approach (generic):**
"Don't get caught without protection. Install an alarm system today."

**Risk reframe (correct):**
"Tonight, after you fall asleep, two men could quietly break your window. Before you're even awake, one of them is in your kids' room. You hear your wife scream from down the hall. And by the time it's over, you're lying there — they've taken everything — feeling like you failed the one job that matters."

The prospect is a father. The identity wound is: I failed to protect my family. That's motivations #1 (protector identity), #3 (guilt), and #14 (inability to defend family) all activated by one scene.

### Children's Bed Guardrail

**Wrong approach:**
"Keep your child safe with our bed rail."

**Risk reframe (correct):**
"Right now, your daughter is sleeping three inches from the edge. During nap time, she rolls. You hear the thud. By the time you reach the room she's unconscious on the floor. In the ambulance you're just holding her and wondering if you missed something obvious that any good mother would have handled."

The identity wound: I'm a terrible mother. That activates #3 (guilt for others' suffering), #1 (good parent identity), and #12 (fear — this time for the child, not the self).

## Why This Works: The Psychology

The prospect already knows at some level that the risk exists. They just haven't confronted it emotionally — they've suppressed the thought because it's uncomfortable.

Copy that brings them into the specific future scenario isn't *creating* fear — it's *surfacing* awareness of something they already live with. Once that awareness is activated, the product becomes an obvious solution to an active problem.

The most powerful proof of this: reading or writing these scenarios triggers the feeling in the writer too. If picturing the scenario makes *you* want to buy the product, it will do the same for the reader.

---

## Research Implications

For preventative products, **standard VOC research will not surface the problem** — because the prospect hasn't felt it and isn't talking about it.

Research instead in:
- **Aftermath communities**: People who've *already had the thing happen* (crime forums, accident support groups, parenting communities after incidents). Their language about what happened gives you the scenario material.
- **Risk-aware communities**: Security enthusiasts, safety-conscious parents, forums where people discuss "it could happen to anyone."
- **News coverage and statistics**: Real incident narratives that give you the raw material for authentic scenarios.
- **The product's negative reviews / 1-star scenarios in reverse**: What did people wish they'd had before? That's the scenario.

The goal: find real, specific narratives of what happens when the risk materializes — and use those as the basis for the dark future scenario in copy.

---

## Important Boundary

**This technique requires that the scenario be real and possible.** You're surfacing genuine risk, not fabricating threats.

The test: Is the bad outcome something that genuinely happens to real people? If yes — you're doing the prospect a favor by making them aware. If you're inventing or exaggerating risks to scare someone into buying — that's manipulation and it erodes trust.

Real statistics, real incident narratives, real testimonials from people who had the thing happen: all of these validate that the risk is genuine.
