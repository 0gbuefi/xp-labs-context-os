# Identity as the Primary Buying Driver

## Core Principle

Identity — the desire to become, affirm, or project a particular self-concept — is almost always the **most powerful** reason anyone buys anything.

It is also almost **never stated directly**.

This combination makes it the single most important thing to look for in market research: it's everywhere, it's the most powerful motivation, and the person will almost never tell you "I want this because of who I want to be."

Your job as a researcher and copywriter is to infer it from the clues.

---

## Why Identity Is So Dominant

People buy to answer the question: **"Who am I? Who do I want to be? Who do I want others to see me as?"**

This is true even when the surface motivation is pain relief, skill acquisition, or status. Layer below — almost always — is identity.

**Examples of identity driving seemingly "non-identity" purchases:**
- Buying a back pain remedy → identity of "active grandparent who can pick up their grandkids"
- Taking a copywriting course → identity of "professional who knows what the experts know"
- Buying a chef's knife → identity of "someone who cooks like a professional chef"
- Giving to charity → identity of "a giving, morally good person"
- Wearing a sports jersey → identity of "loyal fan of this team, part of this community"
- Subscribing to the Wall Street Journal → identity of "the informed, successful person in the know"

---

## Forms Identity Takes in Copy Research

When reading online posts, reviews, and comments, identity signals appear as:

### 1. Skill Identity
"I want to be the kind of person who can do X."
- Fluent Spanish speaker
- Skilled chef
- Professional-grade copywriter
- Strong, fit person

### 2. Intelligence / Knowledge Identity
"I want to be the kind of person who knows things."
- The informed person in the room
- The expert who sees what others miss
- "I want to know what the professionals know"
- Classic angle: magazines, newspapers, courses, membership communities

### 3. Moral / Values Identity
"I want to be the kind of person who lives their values."
- The giving person (charity donations)
- The good parent
- The provider and protector of their family
- The person who stands for something (political merchandise, cause advocacy)

### 4. Affiliation Identity
"I want to be associated with X group, brand, or movement."
- Team/brand/celebrity allegiance
- Professional communities
- Political or cultural movements

### 5. Role Identity
"I want to live up to my role."
- The father who provides and protects
- The breadwinner
- The good mother who invests in herself too
- The leader who leads

### 6. Social Identity
"I want others to perceive me a certain way."
- Admired for strength, intelligence, humor, wealth
- Envied by peers
- Respected in professional context

## How to Surface Identity in Research

Identity is almost never stated as "I want to be X." Look for these signals instead:

**Direct signals (rare but clear):**
- "I want to be the kind of person who..."
- "I've always thought of myself as..."
- "I finally feel like a real [role/identity]"

**Indirect signals (common):**
- Aspirational language about a product ("this is what professionals use")
- References to admired people using the product (Gordon Ramsay's knife)
- Frustration language about falling short of a role ("I should be able to do X")
- Language about proving something to someone (revenge motivation often = identity threat)
- Embarrassment signals (often = threat to identity)
- "I want to be the guy/woman who..."

**Questions to ask yourself as a researcher:**
- Who does this person want to be?
- What identity are they trying to claim, protect, or prove?
- What role do they see themselves in — and how does this purchase help them inhabit that role?

---

## Application to Cold Email

In cold email, identity opens doors more than pain does — but it's subtler to deploy.

**Pain-forward approach (common):**
→ "Struggling to book consistent meetings?"

**Identity-forward approach (more powerful when done well):**
→ "Most founders at your stage are doing [X] — [client outcome] suggests you're thinking about it differently."

Identity says: *this product is for someone like you, the kind of person you already are or want to be.* That's more appealing than pain acknowledgement because it's aspirational rather than critical.

**Identity + pain combo (most effective):**
Identity activates aspiration. Pain activates urgency. When you can show someone that their current situation is a gap between who they are and who they want to be — you get both.
