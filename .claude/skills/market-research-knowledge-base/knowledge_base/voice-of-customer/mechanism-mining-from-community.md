# Mechanism Mining from Community

Beyond language and pain points, online communities sometimes offer an unexpected bonus: **explanatory mechanisms**. These are crowd-sourced explanations of *why* something happens — delivered in plain language by non-experts who happen to be right (or at least believable).

When a comment explaining a mechanism gets hundreds of upvotes, it means the community found it credible and it clicked for them. That credibility transfers to copy. If readers already believe the mechanism (because it's been validated by their own community), you don't have to convince them — you just present it.

This is the "Dr. Reddit" principle: sometimes the most persuasive copy mechanism comes not from scientific literature but from a well-upvoted Reddit comment that gave the audience a framework for understanding their own experience.

## Core Principles

- Mechanisms found in communities are often more persuasive than clinical sources because the audience already trusts them
- High upvotes on a mechanism comment = the community pre-validated this explanation
- A good mechanism creates a "ring of truth" — it feels right because it makes logical sense
- Mechanisms turn copy from sympathy into insight — you're not just describing the pain, you're explaining it
- Always verify if publishing for a professional context; for cold email, the community validation is often sufficient signal

## When to Use

Look for mechanism comments when:
- You have a strong problem description but need a "why does this happen?" explanation
- The copy needs to go deeper than identifying a pain and needs to explain it
- You want to position your solution as the logical next step (problem → mechanism → solution)
- Building a longer-form email or sales page that needs a body section

**Particularly powerful in:**
- Health/wellness markets (physical explanations resonate strongly)
- Business performance markets ("here's why your conversion rate is low")
- Any market where the audience suspects there's a root cause they don't fully understand

## How to Apply

### Identifying Mechanism Comments

Look for comments that:
- Begin with "the reason..." / "this happens because..." / "it's actually..." / "turns out..."
- Explain a process or cause-and-effect
- Have high upvote counts relative to other comments in the thread
- Generate "that makes so much sense" replies below them

### Validating the Mechanism

A good mechanism for copy purposes needs:
1. **Plausibility:** Does it make intuitive sense even without evidence?
2. **Community validation:** Did it get upvoted (meaning readers found it credible)?
3. **Alignment with the problem:** Does it explain the exact pain you're writing about?

You don't always need formal verification for cold email — if it's a well-known mechanism (like cortisol in the morning), common knowledge is enough. If it's a novel or controversial claim, verify before using.

### Using the Mechanism in Copy

Structure: **Problem → Mechanism → Solution**

1. Agitate the problem using their language (staying close to their words)
2. Introduce the mechanism: "Turns out, this happens because..."
3. Present your solution as the logical fix for the mechanism

The mechanism creates the bridge between "I have this problem" and "and here's how your product fixes it" — without the bridge feeling like a hard sell.
