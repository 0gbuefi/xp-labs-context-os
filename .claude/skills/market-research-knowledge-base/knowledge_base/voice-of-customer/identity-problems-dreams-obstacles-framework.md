# Identity, Problems, Dreams, Obstacles Framework

When reading online posts, reviews, and comments during market research, most copywriters don't know what to look for — so they collect vague impressions instead of usable data. This framework solves that by giving you four specific buckets to fill as you read.

Every piece of audience language falls into one of these four categories. By consciously sorting findings into each bucket, you end up with copy-ready material that covers the full emotional landscape of the buyer.

## Core Principles

- **Identity:** How they see themselves, their role, their values, their in-group membership
- **Problems:** The specific pain they're experiencing right now — exact language matters, not paraphrases
- **Dreams:** What they want life/work to look like — the outcome they're buying toward
- **Obstacles:** What's stood between them and their dream — what they've tried, why it failed

These four buckets map directly to cold email copy elements:
- Identity → opening line personalization ("as someone who...")
- Problems → pain-agitation, poke-the-bear
- Dreams → value prop, outcome framing
- Obstacles → objection handling, positioning against alternatives

## When to Use

Use this framework whenever:
- Reading Reddit threads, forum posts, Amazon reviews, or YouTube comments
- Processing any raw audience research data
- Building out a client's pain point knowledge base
- Creating copy angles for a new campaign
- Briefing a writer on the audience

## How to Apply

### Bucket 1: Identity Language

What clues does their language give about how they see themselves?
- Job titles and roles they claim ("as a solo founder...", "being a sales manager...")
- Values they express ("I care about...", "I believe...")
- In-group membership ("we bootstrapped founders...", "people like us...")
- How they describe their situation ("I'm at the stage where...")

**Why it matters for copy:** Opening lines that reflect identity back to the reader feel instantly relevant. "You're growing a team without an HR department" speaks directly to an identity.

### Bucket 2: Problems Language

What do they say their problem actually is — in their words?
- Direct complaints and frustrations
- Specific scenarios where the problem shows up ("every time I try to...")
- Comparisons to how things should be ("it shouldn't be this hard to...")
- Emotional language around the problem (exhausted, overwhelmed, stuck, embarrassed)

**Why it matters for copy:** The poke-the-bear email element requires knowing exactly how to name the pain in the language the prospect uses themselves.

### Bucket 3: Dreams Language

What does success look like to them?
- The outcome they're explicitly hoping for
- What "finally solving this" would mean for their life or work
- How they describe the ideal state
- What they celebrate when they find something that works

**Why it matters for copy:** Value propositions that reflect the actual dream (not the assumed dream) land harder. "Double your pipeline" hits differently if they've said "I just want consistent lead flow."

### Bucket 4: Obstacles Language

What's gotten in their way?
- Things they've already tried
- Why previous solutions failed
- What they're skeptical about
- The specific friction points they name

**Why it matters for copy:** Objection handling and positioning against alternatives. If they've tried tool X and it failed, you can position against that without naming it.

### Bonus: Journey Stories

Also note: what stories do they tell about how they got here?
- "I used to [old way], then [trigger], now I'm trying to [new solution]"
- These journey narratives reveal the trigger event that made them open to change — and that trigger is a campaign angle
