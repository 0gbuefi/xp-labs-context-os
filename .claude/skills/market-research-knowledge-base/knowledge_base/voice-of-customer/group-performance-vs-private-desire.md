# Group Performance vs. Private Desire

Anonymous platforms reduce social performance — but they don't eliminate it when people are talking *to each other* within a community. A user in r/FemaleDataStrategy performing "I'm done with men" for her peers is still performing. The community has its own identity and norms, and members speak to maintain their standing in it.

The copywriter's target is not what someone says to their community. It's what they're secretly thinking when they close Reddit and lie in bed at night.

**The tell:** When someone writes "I wrote this as much for myself as anyone else" — they've dropped the performance mask briefly. They've admitted the post is internal monologue dressed up as advice. That's your copy.

## Core Principles

- Communities create identity norms — members perform for each other even on anonymous platforms
- The performance is the publicly stated position; the private desire is underneath
- Strong group performances (mass agreement, rallying language) reveal widely shared private desires
- The original poster often breaks the mask briefly — look for moments of personal admission within performative posts
- Anonymous ≠ no social performance. It means less social performance than named platforms.

## When to Use

Apply this framework when:
- A community's stated position seems to contradict what the product offers
- Research surfaces a lot of "we don't need X" or "X is bad" community consensus
- You're trying to understand the gap between what the audience says and what they want
- Writing objection-handling copy (addressing the public position to reach the private desire)

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Identify the Performance Layer

What is this community's shared identity? What do they perform?
- "We're strong, independent women who don't need men"
- "We're too smart to fall for marketing"
- "We've moved past [desire the product serves]"

This is the public face. It's not useless — it tells you how they want to be seen, which is useful for framing copy (don't trigger the identity).

### Step 2: Find the Mask Slips

Look for moments in posts or comments where the performance breaks:
- Personal admission language: "I wrote this for myself too," "I needed to hear this," "honestly I feel this too"
- Questions asked in spite of the stated position: "But does anyone know how to..."
- The anxious internal checklist inside the dismissive post: listing the exact fears they're supposedly dismissing

### Step 3: Read the Anxiety Behind the Bravado

The most useful signal: what anxieties does the post attempt to dismiss?
- "Stop checking if he saw your story" = they're definitely checking if he saw their story
- "Don't double text" = they desperately want to double text
- "He's not busy, he just doesn't want to" = they've been telling themselves he's busy

Every instruction NOT to do something in a community context is a confession that the community is doing exactly that thing.

### Step 4: Target the Private Desire in Copy

Write to the private version, not the public performance:
- Don't say: "Ladies, you deserve better than this" (validates the performance)
- Do say: "You've already checked if he saw your message. Twice." (speaks to the private truth)

Bypassing the performance and speaking directly to the private experience creates the "how do you know me?" recognition that makes copy convert.
