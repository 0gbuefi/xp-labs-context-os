# Community Post as Copy Mirror

The highest form of voice-of-customer research is finding a post so emotionally precise, so resonant with the audience (as evidenced by upvotes), that it already functions as copy. The audience member who wrote it was essentially writing your copy for you — articulating the internal monologue of the market in the market's own language.

When you find such a post, your job is not to extract data from it. Your job is to **mirror its structure** — strip the specific framing that doesn't serve the product, keep the emotional arc, redirect the conclusion toward the solution.

The result is copy that feels impossibly intimate because it was literally written by a member of the audience you're writing for.

## Core Principles

- High-upvote posts = the community confirmed "this is exactly how we feel"
- The post's structure (problem → agitation → conclusion) often maps directly to PAS copy structure
- You mirror the arc, not the words verbatim — change enough to avoid plagiarism, keep the emotional precision
- Internal monologue posts ("I wrote this for myself") are especially valuable — they're literally unfiltered inner voice
- The post's specific details (the exact anxious questions, the exact rationalizations) become your copy's most resonant moments

## When to Use

Use the community-post-as-copy-mirror technique when:
- You find a highly upvoted post that is emotionally precise about the target audience's experience
- The post structures around the same problem your product solves
- You're writing an email or long-form copy that needs a strong, specific hook
- You want the opening section of copy to feel like it's reading the reader's mind

**Best indicators that a post can be mirrored:**
- 500+ upvotes
- Replies saying "this is exactly what I needed to hear" / "how did you know?"
- The post author admits they wrote it for themselves
- The post contains a list of specific anxieties or internal questions

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Identify the Emotional Arc

Map the post's structure:
- What situation does it open with?
- What internal experience does it describe?
- What conclusion does it reach?

For the dating example:
- Opens: He's not texting you back
- Middle: Here are all the excuses you're making (and why they're wrong) + all the signs confirming he saw your message
- Conclusion: Stop giving him the benefit of the doubt (this is the performance — you replace it)

### Step 2: Extract the Specific Details

The specific details are the gold:
- "Did he see your snap? Did he see your posts? Did he read your text? Is he lost at sea?"
- These specific anxious questions are what makes the copy feel like mind-reading

Generic pain: "You might be wondering why he hasn't texted back"
Specific mirror: "You've checked if he saw your story. You've checked if he read your message. You've been telling yourself he's busy."

### Step 3: Replace the Conclusion

The original post's conclusion serves the post author's goal (community identity), not your goal (sell the solution). Replace it:
- Original conclusion: "Be better, expect better, stop wasting your time"
- Copy conclusion: "What if you knew how to turn the tables? What if you could make him feel the insecurity of you not texting him back?"

### Step 4: Mirror the Language, Don't Copy It

Reword enough to avoid plagiarism:
- "Slaps me straight in my eyeball" → "hits you right in the face"
- "He's not busy, he just doesn't want to" → keep this, it's too precise to improve
- Flip second-person (the post says "you" talking down) to third-person ("he") where it redirects the focus
