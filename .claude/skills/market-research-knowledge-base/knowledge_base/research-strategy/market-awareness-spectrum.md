# Market Awareness Spectrum

## Core Principle

**Every product solves a problem. But markets will have different levels of awareness of what that problem is.**

The awareness level of your market determines:
1. What research will find (and what it won't)
2. How to frame copy
3. What problem you're actually selling against

Failing to diagnose awareness level before researching leads to misreading what you find — or looking in the wrong places entirely.

---

## The Spectrum

### Stage 1: Unaware
The market doesn't know they have a problem. They may not even know the product category exists.

**Examples:**
- Home alarm systems (buyer hasn't been broken into; doesn't feel a problem)
- Children's bed guardrails (parent hasn't experienced a child falling; no felt need)
- Insurance (no incident has occurred)
- Preventative health screenings (no symptoms yet)

**What research looks like at this stage:**
- Little to no language about the product or its direct benefits
- No search behavior for solutions (they're not looking)
- Indirect signals: fear language about related risks (crime in area, stories of others' accidents)
- Conversations about the consequences if things go wrong — not about the product itself

**Copy implication:** You cannot address a problem they don't feel. You must first create awareness of the risk they're carrying. See [[preventative-product-risk-reframe]].

---

### Stage 2: Problem-Aware
The market knows they have a problem but may not know solutions exist or be actively looking.

**Examples:**
- Chronic back pain sufferer who's "just living with it"
- Sales manager who knows their pipeline is weak but hasn't sought a fix
- Business owner who suspects their emails aren't working but hasn't researched why

**What research looks like at this stage:**
- Rich pain language (the [[identity-problems-dreams-obstacles-framework]] works well here)
- Frustration posts, venting threads, "does anyone else feel this" discussions
- Searching for validation that the problem is real and common
- Little search behavior for the specific solution yet

**Copy implication:** Acknowledge and agitate the problem. Lead with what they already feel. The [[specific-situation-beats-generic-pain]] pattern is especially effective here.

---

### Stage 3: Solution-Aware
The market knows solutions exist and is actively comparing options.

**Examples:**
- SaaS buyer evaluating CRMs
- Someone who's decided to fix their back pain and is researching options
- B2B buyer doing vendor shortlisting

**What research looks like at this stage:**
- Comparison language ("X vs. Y", "which is better for...")
- Feature/capability discussions
- Reviews and objection language (what's stopped them from buying each option)
- Already-bought communities debating tradeoffs

**Copy implication:** Lead with differentiation, not problem. They know the problem — they need to understand why this solution over alternatives.

---

## Research Implications by Stage

| Awareness Stage | Where to Research | What to Look For | Copy Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unaware | News, crime forums, parenting communities, risk-adjacent conversations | Fear language, consequence stories, "I never thought about…" | Risk reframe — surface the unprotected risk as their current problem |
| Problem-Aware | Reddit, forums, reviews, complaint threads | Pain language, situation narratives, dream language | Problem-led, situational, agitation-heavy |
| Solution-Aware | Review sites, comparison communities, subreddits for the category | Objections, comparisons, feature debates | Differentiation-led, objection-handling |

## The B2B / Cold Email Application

Most B2B cold email targets are **problem-aware at best** — and in many technical or operational categories, prospects are closer to **unaware** because:
- They've normalized the inefficiency ("that's just how it works here")
- They don't know a solution exists for their specific situation
- The problem lives in a team/system they don't have direct visibility into

**Research signal:** If prospects use language like "we've just always done it this way" or "I didn't realize that was fixable" — you're dealing with a partially unaware market, even in B2B.

**Approach:** You may need to bring them to awareness of the problem (the consequence of not solving it) before introducing your solution. This is more top-of-funnel education than traditional cold email — but it's the honest approach for unaware markets.

---

## Diagnostic Questions for a New Client

When starting research for a new client, diagnose awareness level first:

1. Does their ICP *know* they have the problem the product solves?
2. Are they actively searching for solutions, or not yet searching?
3. When you read their online communities — are they discussing the problem, or are they not discussing it at all?
4. Has the product category been around long enough that people know it exists?

Answers determine where to look and what copy frame to use.
