# Three-Step Introverted Research Process

The core market research process for mining online communities requires no direct human contact — everything is done by reading what people have already written publicly. It's called "introverted research" because you observe rather than intervene.

The process is intentionally simple: find where they talk, read what they say, write it down. The complexity lives inside each step — knowing *which* platforms to use, *what* to look for, and *when* you have enough. But the skeleton is always these three steps.

**Important:** This is a messy, non-linear process in practice. Don't expect it to feel clean and ordered. Follow the threads, go down rabbit holes, come back up, find new threads.

## Core Principles

- No human contact required — purely observational, asynchronous research
- The process is the same regardless of platform (Reddit, Amazon, YouTube, forums)
- Messiness is normal — follow the threads wherever they go
- Document continuously, not after the fact — note things as you find them
- Stop when you stop finding new things (saturation), not on a timer

## When to Use

Use this process whenever:
- Starting research on a new client's audience
- Validating an ICP hypothesis with real-world evidence
- Mining for copy language before writing cold email campaigns
- Needing to understand a market you know nothing about
- Building out a client's knowledge base with authentic audience language

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Find Your Audience

Locate where the target market congregates online:
- Search Reddit for subreddits related to their problem or identity
- Search for forums in the niche (Google: "[niche] forum")
- Find relevant books on Amazon and identify their review sections
- Locate YouTube channels/videos targeting this audience
- Ask the client: "Where does your audience hang out online?"

**Output:** A list of specific URLs/communities to research (e.g., r/smallbusiness, specific forum thread, Amazon book ASIN)

### Step 2: Read and Note

Systematically read through the content, extracting signals. For each source, look for:
- How they describe their **identity** (who they see themselves as)
- How they describe their **problems** (exact language, not paraphrases)
- What their **dreams** look like (what success means to them)
- What **obstacles** they name (what's stood in their way)
- What **stories** they tell (how they got here, what they've tried)
- What **journey** they describe (first discovering the problem → where they are now)

Take notes as you go — don't batch it. The act of noting is the research.

### Step 3: Document Until Saturation

Keep going until you stop finding genuinely new things. Write everything on a running document (the "customer zooka doc" or equivalent):
- Direct quotes with source URL
- Recurring themes and phrases
- Surprising or counterintuitive findings
- Language patterns and vocabulary

**Saturation signal:** You're reading new posts/reviews/comments but nothing is surprising you anymore. The same themes keep recurring. You can predict what the next comment will say. That's when you stop.
