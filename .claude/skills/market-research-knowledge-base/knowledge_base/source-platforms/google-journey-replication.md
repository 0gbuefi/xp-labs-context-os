# Google Journey Replication

One of the most powerful research techniques is to literally walk in the buyer's shoes: search the keywords they would search, click the results they would click, join the email lists they would join, and see the ads being served to them. This replicates the prospect's journey from "I have this problem" to "I found a potential solution" — and everything in between is research data.

This technique surfaces competitive intelligence, industry language, and the existing offers the market has already been exposed to — all of which inform how to position and differentiate your client's messaging.

## Core Principles

- Search the way the prospect searches, not the way an expert searches
- Use beginner/layperson terminology, not industry jargon, in your searches
- Every click is a data point: what sites rank? What ads run? What do those pages say?
- Email lists reveal ongoing messaging patterns — what tone, what offers, what sequences
- Top-ranking paid ads represent what competitors are paying to say — high-signal competitive intelligence
- You are mapping the existing information landscape the prospect navigates before buying

## When to Use

Use Google journey replication when:
- Starting research on any market where search intent is high
- Wanting to understand competitive messaging and positioning
- Need to see what offers and CTAs the audience has already been exposed to
- Looking for blog/forum content that's driving traffic in the space
- Want to understand how the market talks about the problem in search terms

## How to Apply

**Step 1: Build keyword list**
- Start with the most basic, non-expert version of the problem search
- Add variations: "how to [solve problem]", "[problem] help", "best [solution]", "[problem] vs [alternative]"
- Ask the client: "What would someone Google the day they first realized they had this problem?"

**Step 2: Execute the journey**
- Search each keyword and note the top organic results and top paid ads
- Click through to 3-5 top results and read the page — what angle are they using?
- Click on ads — what offers are being made? What's the CTA?
- Sign up for email lists you encounter — capture the welcome sequence and ongoing emails
- Note language patterns used across multiple competing sites

**Step 3: Go down rabbit holes intentionally**
- Follow links between related sites — these cross-references reveal the ecosystem
- Reddit often surfaces in Google results; treat these as a bridge to further Reddit research
- Industry blogs often have comment sections — check these too

**Step 4: Document findings**
- Competitive offers and positioning angles
- Language patterns appearing across multiple sources
- Gaps in the market — what nobody is saying that the audience probably wants to hear
