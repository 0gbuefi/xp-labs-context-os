# Audience: Creative Envy and Comparison

Creative envy operates on two levels — looking up at legends and feeling a gut-punch of inadequacy, and looking sideways at peers with clever ideas you wish you'd thought of. Crucially, most copywriters on a given list DON'T relate to this. Throssell estimates it's a minority.

But for those who DO feel it, it's one of the most powerful emotional triggers — it separates the craftspeople from the mercenaries ("just tell me the copy templates that make money").

## Audience Language

- "I want to quit copywriting" (the dramatic expression of comparison despair)
- "any true artist knows the despair of looking at something incredible someone else has created"
- "Why couldn't _I_ be the one to create that?"
- "AND IT'S JUST NOT FAIR THAT SOMEONE CAN BE SO GOOD"
- "just tell me the copy templates that make money" (what the NON-creative copywriters say)

## Evidence

[VERIFIED: Email, "I want to quit copywriting" by Daniel Throssell (May 17, 2022)] Creative envy as a minority but intense experience. The split between copywriters who feel craft pain and those who just want templates.

## The Reframe

The envy is not purely destructive — Throssell offers a resolution: "You have to feel this way... But, and here's the kicker... If you want to be truly great... You then need to tear your eyes away from their work for long enough to create your own thing." The envy is a signal of caring about craft, which is a positive force when channeled into original work.

## How This Informs Campaigns

This identifies a psychographic segment within XP Labs's ICP — the craft-driven copywriters vs. the template-followers. Campaign angles:
- Craft-driven copywriters will respond to "find the perfect phrase" messaging
- Template-followers will respond to "save time" messaging
- XP Labs can serve both but with different hooks
- Creative envy → "Your copy will have language no one else is using" (exclusivity angle)
